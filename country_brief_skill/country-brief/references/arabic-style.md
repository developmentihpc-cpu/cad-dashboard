# Arabic Style Guide — Country Briefs

This document codifies the language conventions for Arabic-version country briefs. The goal is **professional textbook Arabic** (الفصحى المعاصرة الأكاديمية), not journalism Arabic and not machine-translated Arabic. A reader with native Arabic fluency in a professional setting (finance, development, foreign policy, academia) should find the brief indistinguishable from native institutional analytical writing — the register modeled on IMF Article IV Arabic editions, World Bank Arabic country pages, Brookings Doha publications, and Crisis Group Arabic.

## Production discipline: three-phase

The earlier "compose natively in Arabic, not translated" rule was aspirational fiction — it pretended that Claude's Arabic analytical depth matches its English. It doesn't. Training-data distribution is heavily English-weighted, and pure native Arabic composition produced thinner briefs than the English equivalents. The revised discipline is honest about this asymmetry and produces stronger output:

**Phase 1 — Analytical composition in English.** Produce the full brief in English at full depth from source research. This is where the analytical heavy lifting happens — section selection, chart picks, source synthesis, judgment calibration, decision-implication framing. The English version becomes the analytical and structural scaffold.

**Phase 2 — Translation with rhetorical adaptation.** Translate to Arabic at the *paragraph* level, never sentence by sentence. Sentence-by-sentence translation produces calques and English-syntax-in-Arabic — that's the failure mode the rule must prevent. Paragraph-level adaptation preserves the analytical content while restructuring prose for Arabic rhetoric: verbal vs. nominal sentence patterns, connector choice (إذ، حيث، بحيث، وعليه), signposting conventions, hedging elaboration, calibrated rhythm. The proper-name register and glossary apply throughout for terminology consistency.

**Phase 3 — Editorial pass against Arabic institutional reference style.** When a sentence reads as stilted, too journalistic, or recognizably translated, check the phrasing against analogous content from institutional Arabic sources and adjust toward the institutional norm. The reference sources to check against:

- **IMF Arabic publications** — Article IV consultations, WEO regional chapters, FATF guidance. Gold standard for macroeconomic and financial Arabic. The right model for risk language, debt language, balance-of-payments framing.
- **World Bank Arabic country pages and CEMs** — development, governance, social-indicator Arabic.
- **Arab Monetary Fund (صندوق النقد العربي)** publications — Gulf financial Arabic, peer-comparison framing.
- **Brookings Doha Center / AGSI / Carnegie Middle East** Arabic content — political analysis register.
- **Al-Sharq Al-Awsat** business and commentary sections — register reference for professional but accessible analytical prose.
- **Crisis Group Arabic** — conflict, sanctions, security-analysis register.

The editorial pass is targeted, not blanket: read the brief sentence by sentence and flag anything that feels off, then check the flagged sentences against an institutional reference. Most paragraphs need no adjustment; the ones that do get a precise fix.

**Quality bar.** A native Arabic reader at IMF / World Bank / Brookings-level professional fluency should find the brief indistinguishable from native institutional analytical writing in their domain. Not from native journalism, not from classical scholarship — from native *institutional* analytical writing.

## Register

Use **Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) at the academic-professional register** (الفصحى المعاصرة، السجل الأكاديمي-المهني). Reference points:

- **Right register:** Arabic-language IMF Article IV reports, Arab Monetary Fund publications, Brookings Doha Center research papers, academic journals from Qatar University / KAUST / AUB, professional analysis in Al-Sharq Al-Awsat or Asharq Business commentary sections.
- **Wrong register (too casual):** Gulf newspaper Arabic, Al Jazeera headlines, Twitter Arabic, mixed Arabic-English business jargon.
- **Wrong register (too archaic):** Classical Arabic syntax (متشعّب الجمل بأسلوب الأقدمين), heavy use of rare lexical items, religious or literary registers.

The brief is **professional analysis written for educated decision-makers**, not journalism for general audiences and not classical scholarship for specialists.

## Sentence structure and rhetoric

**Prefer balanced, well-formed sentences over very short or very long ones.** A typical sentence in this brief is 15-30 words. Avoid both staccato bullet-rhythm and run-on accumulation.

**Verbal vs nominal sentences.** Use verbal sentences (الجملة الفعلية) for narrative and analytical claims where the verb carries the analytical work: *تشهد إيران أعمق أزمة منذ عام 1979*. Use nominal sentences (الجملة الاسمية) for state-of-affairs description and definitional claims: *الاقتصاد الإيراني في حالة تدهور متسارع*. Strong writing alternates both rather than defaulting to one structure.

**Use formal connectors and signposting.** Arabic professional writing uses more explicit transitions than English. Prefer:
- إذ (since/given that) for causal links
- حيث (where/whereas) for relative-clause analytical refinement
- بحيث (such that) for consequence
- وعليه, ومن ثَمّ (and thus, therefore) for conclusions
- ومن الجدير بالملاحظة, تجدر الإشارة إلى أن (notably, it should be noted that) for emphasis
- من جهة... من جهة أخرى (on one hand... on the other) for balanced presentation
- في المقابل (by contrast)
- علاوة على ذلك, فضلاً عن ذلك (furthermore, in addition)
- لكن (but) and بيد أن (however) — لكن is acceptable in this register but بيد أن is more formal

**Avoid:**
- و repeated as the only connector (sentence after sentence joined by و reads as oral, not written)
- لأن used to begin a sentence (use إذ or نظراً لأن)
- Excessive use of فإن in news-style emphatic constructions

**Confidence and calibration language** should be more elaborated than English's terse tags:
- "high confidence" → بدرجة عالية من الثقة / بثقة عالية / بدرجة كبيرة من اليقين
- "moderate confidence" → بدرجة معتدلة من الثقة / بدرجة متوسطة من اليقين
- "low confidence" → بدرجة منخفضة من الثقة / مع تحفظات منهجية كبيرة
- "war-contingent" → مرهون بمسار الحرب / مشروط بتطورات الصراع

**Hedging conventions.** Arabic professional writing prefers slightly more elaborated hedging than English:
- "may" / "might" → قد / يُحتمل أن / من المرجح أن / من غير المستبعد أن
- "appears to" → يبدو أن / يُظهر أن / تشير المؤشرات إلى أن
- "suggests" → يُشير إلى / يُلمح إلى / يُوحي بأن

## Lexicon and terminology

**Default to established Arabic terms over loanwords.** Where Arabic has a well-established equivalent, use it:

| English / loanword | Use this Arabic | Don't use |
|---|---|---|
| portfolio | المحفظة الاستثمارية | البورتفوليو |
| stakeholder | الأطراف المعنية / أصحاب المصلحة | الستيكهولدرز |
| infrastructure | البنية التحتية | الإنفراستركتشر |
| compliance | الامتثال | الكومبلاينس |
| due diligence | العناية الواجبة / التحقق الواجب | الديو ديليجنس |
| sovereign default | التخلّف السيادي عن السداد | الديفولت السيادي |
| counterparty | الطرف المقابل | الكاونتر بارتي |
| restructuring | إعادة الهيكلة | الريستركتشرنغ |

**Where the loanword is established and Arabic alternatives feel forced, use the loanword.** Some English terms are now part of professional Arabic and shouldn't be artificially Arabized:

- COP28 → COP28 (not مؤتمر الأطراف الثامن والعشرين except on first mention with the English in parentheses)
- IMF → صندوق النقد الدولي (well-established Arabic)
- IRGC → الحرس الثوري الإيراني (well-established Arabic)
- BIT → اتفاقية حماية الاستثمار الثنائية (Arabic preferred; acronym in parentheses on first use)
- SOFA → اتفاقية وضع القوات (Arabic preferred; acronym in parentheses on first use)
- ESG → الحوكمة البيئية والاجتماعية (Arabic preferred; acronym in parentheses on first use)
- SIDS → الدول الجزرية الصغيرة النامية (Arabic established)

**Numbers:** Use Western-Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3) for statistical and financial data, percentages, dates, and any context where precise quantitative communication matters. Arabic-Indic numerals (١، ٢، ٣) can be used for narrative-paragraph references but the brief defaults to Western-Arabic for consistency with international financial-data conventions. Mixing within a paragraph is acceptable when contextually clear.

**Dates:** Gregorian dates by default (مايو 2026 / أيار 2026). Use both forms with first reference where appropriate (مايو/أيار 2026). Hijri dates only when the source material uses them or when Hijri context is analytically relevant (Iranian Islamic-calendar fiscal year for example).

**Currency:** Spell out on first use, abbreviate after: الدولار الأمريكي (USD), الدرهم الإماراتي (AED), الريال الإيراني (IRR), الدولار الفيجي (FJD). Numerical figures use Western-Arabic numerals: 1.6 إلى 1.8 مليون ريال إيراني للدولار الواحد.

## Proper names and transliterations

**Use the standard Arabic-press form** of each name. When multiple forms exist (e.g., Pezeshkian rendered as بزشكيان or پزشكيان in different sources), pick one and use it consistently throughout the brief.

The companion file `arabic-names.md` maintains a per-country register of standardized names. Add a name to that file when first encountered; subsequent briefs reuse the standardized form.

**Country names:**
- Use the standard short Arabic name in body text: إيران, سريلانكا, فيجي
- Use the formal Arabic name on the cover and in the first reference: جمهورية إيران الإسلامية, جمهورية سريلانكا الديمقراطية الاشتراكية, جمهورية فيجي
- "United Arab Emirates" = الإمارات العربية المتحدة (formal) / الإمارات (short)

**Institutional names:**
- International institutions use their established Arabic names: صندوق النقد الدولي, البنك الدولي, الأمم المتحدة, الوكالة الدولية للطاقة الذرية
- National institutions: use the country's official Arabic name if it has one; otherwise Arabize the institution name consistently (مصرف فيجي المركزي, البنك المركزي الإيراني, لجنة فيجي المستقلة لمكافحة الفساد)

## Layout and direction

**The brief is right-to-left (RTL).** Page layout, paragraph alignment, table column ordering, list bullet positioning, footnote markers, and figure positioning all reverse from the English version.

**Chart typography on systems without Amiri.** Matplotlib renders chart axis labels and tick labels as PNG, which means it uses the system font directly (unlike body text, which goes through WeasyPrint and shapes Arabic via the CSS font-stack). The renderer probes a chain — Amiri → Noto Naskh Arabic → Noto Sans Arabic → Arial → Tahoma → Segoe UI — and uses the first installed font. Windows always ships with Arial which has full Arabic glyph coverage, so chart labels render correctly out of the box even without Amiri. For print-quality typography matching the body, install Amiri. See `INSTALL.md` for the per-OS install path.

**Mixed-direction content** (Arabic text with embedded English company names, currency codes, or acronyms) uses Unicode bidirectional handling. The renderer wraps embedded Latin-script content appropriately. Common cases:

- Company/organization names: keep in original Latin script (BlackRock, Hapag-Lloyd) unless the entity has an established Arabic form
- Currency codes (USD, EUR, AED, IRR): keep in Latin script for consistency with financial conventions
- Person names: use Arabic transliteration with English in parentheses on first reference for traceability: مسعود بزشكيان (Pezeshkian)
- Acronyms (IMF, NATO, COP28): Arabic terms in body text; English acronyms acceptable when widely recognized

**Quotation conventions:**
- Direct quotes from English-language sources: translate to Arabic with attribution, note original language. *قال السفير الأمريكي إن "الموقف الخليجي يجب أن يكون واضحاً" [بحسب التصريح الأصلي بالإنجليزية].*
- Direct quotes from Arabic-language sources: render in original Arabic, attribute normally
- Short technical quotes (single words, specific phrases): can be kept in English with Arabic gloss

## Component-specific guidance

**Verdict strip:** Each tile label is in Arabic; the colored-dot color category stays as the same word (`green`, `amber`, `red`) in the source markdown for renderer recognition. The caption text is Arabic.

**Key judgments:** Arrow + bold claim + parenthetical confidence + analytical support. Confidence language uses the elaborated forms above. Example:
> ↓ **تشهد إيران أعمق أزمة دولة منذ عام 1979** *(بدرجة عالية من الثقة).* جاء مقتل المرشد علي خامنئي في غارة عسكرية أجنبية، وتولّي الحرس الثوري الوظائف التشغيلية للدولة، وحجم الأضرار الذي تقدّره طهران بين 300 مليار ومتريليون دولار، وظروف الحصار البحري، لتجعل من هذه الأزمة فئة مختلفة كلياً عن أي أزمة سابقة في تاريخ الجمهورية الإسلامية.

**Counterfactual block:** Each item names a specific event or trigger, not a generic hedge. Use الشرط structure where natural: *لو [حدث X] لتغيّرت الأحكام...*

**Decision-implication callouts:** Direct, prescriptive language appropriate for decision-makers. Use جملة فعلية imperative-adjacent constructions: *ينبغي على المحفظة الإماراتية تأجيل أي التزام مباشر جديد...*

**Scenario boxes:** Probability + scenario name in the label span; narrative prose in Arabic with explicit conditional structure. Use الشرط البياني patterns: *في حال [استمر الوقف]، فإن...*

**Risk matrix:** Risk labels in Arabic; likelihood and impact words are translated consistently:
- likelihood: low / medium / high / very high → احتمال منخفض / متوسط / عالٍ / عالٍ جداً
- impact: low / medium / high / critical → تأثير منخفض / متوسط / عالٍ / حرج

**Recommendations:** Imperative or jussive forms; Owner/Timeline labels in italics, in Arabic:
- "Owner" → *المسؤول:*
- "Timeline" → *الإطار الزمني:*

**Footnotes and bibliography:** Footnote markers appear after the relevant Arabic phrase (RTL-positioned). Bibliography entries maintain source-language metadata: English sources have their titles in English (sometimes with Arabic gloss); Arabic sources have Arabic titles; mixed sources note both.

## Common analytical traps in Arabic version

**Trap 1: Sentence-by-sentence translation.** When Phase 2 collapses into literal one-to-one translation, the Arabic syntax follows English word order. Signs: prepositional phrases in unnatural positions, definite-article patterns that mirror English, verb-noun agreement that follows English number rather than Arabic dual/plural distinctions, awkward connector chains (و and و and و instead of إذ / حيث / بحيث / وعليه). Discipline: translate at the *paragraph* level. Read the English paragraph, internalize its analytical claim, then write a new Arabic paragraph that makes the same claim using Arabic rhetorical patterns. Read aloud after writing; if it sounds like a translation, mark it for the Phase 3 editorial pass and check against an institutional reference.

**Trap 2: Pseudo-formality.** Substituting rare lexical items or archaic constructions to "sound textbook" produces stilted prose. Textbook Arabic is formal but readable, not ornamental. If a sentence requires a dictionary to parse, it's wrong register.

**Trap 3: Inconsistent terminology.** The same concept rendered three different ways across the brief (e.g., "sanctions" as العقوبات in one section and الحظر in another). The glossary exists to prevent this. Use it.

**Trap 4: Mistranslated names.** Pezeshkian written two different ways in two sections; "Bainimarama" rendered inconsistently. The proper-name register exists to prevent this. Use it.

**Trap 5: Cultural-context flattening.** English brief says "Hijab politics" with specific compressed meaning. Arabic version that says سياسات الحجاب without context flattens meaningful local distinctions. Better: سياسات الحجاب الإلزامي in Iranian context; specific terminology for Saudi or other contexts.

**Trap 6: Hedge inflation.** English's "moderate confidence" rendered as بدرجة متوسطة من الثقة is appropriate; but stringing additional hedges (بدرجة متوسطة من الثقة مع تحفظات منهجية كبيرة وفي ضوء محدودية البيانات) becomes evasive. One layer of hedging matching the English calibration; not multiple stacked hedges.

## Quality bar

A native Arabic-reading professional in finance, foreign policy, or development should find the Arabic brief:
- Readable at normal professional reading pace (not requiring re-reading paragraphs)
- Indistinguishable from native Arabic professional analysis on any specific sentence
- Consistent in terminology across all sections
- Free of obvious translation artifacts (calques, false friends, English-syntax-in-Arabic)
- Appropriately calibrated in confidence and hedging
- Faithful to the analytical content of the English version without being mechanically parallel to it

If any of these conditions fails, the brief is not yet at production quality. Iterate.
