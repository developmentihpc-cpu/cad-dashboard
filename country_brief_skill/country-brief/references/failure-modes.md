# Common Failure Modes in Country Briefs

Country briefs fail in predictable ways. Most quality problems aren't about missing facts — they're about how those facts get assembled. The failure modes below are drawn from intelligence-community tradecraft literature, academic work on analytic bias, and patterns visible in mediocre country reports across the development, investment, and policy worlds.

For each failure mode: what it is, how to spot it in a draft, and how to fix it.

---

## 1. Mirror imaging

**What it is.** Assuming actors in the target country reason the way the analyst (or the analyst's institution) does. The classic example: Western analysts assuming that an authoritarian leader will respond to economic pressure the way a Western politician would — by softening to preserve electoral support — when their actual incentive structure is regime survival, which may point the opposite direction.

**Spot it.** Draft sentences like "the regime will likely concede to avoid further sanctions pressure" or "the government will prioritize economic stability over [other goal]." The analyst is projecting their own model of rationality.

**Fix.** For every major actor, ask: what is this actor's actual incentive structure, and what does their past behavior reveal about how they weigh trade-offs? Cite specific past decisions, not assumed motivations. Where local-language sources or area expertise is available, use it.

---

## 2. Stale anchors

**What it is.** Building analysis on facts that were true 3–10 years ago but are no longer accurate. This is especially common when the analyst remembers a country from prior reading and doesn't refresh on the present. Political turnover, economic transformation, and conflict dynamics can change country pictures dramatically in a short period.

**Spot it.** Statistics without dates. References to "the current government" without naming when it took power. Phrasings like "in recent years" attached to events from 2018.

**Fix.** Every statistic gets a date and a source. Any data older than 3 years gets flagged explicitly ("World Bank, 2021 — most recent available"). For political claims, name the date of the event and verify it's still the current state.

---

## 3. Single-source dependency

**What it is.** Leaning on one outlet, one think tank, or one government agency for most of the brief. The hidden problem: that source's bias becomes the brief's bias.

**Spot it.** Footnotes are dominated by one institution. Or — even worse — the bibliography looks diverse but a single source provides the load-bearing claims for most major judgments.

**Fix.** Triangulate. For every major analytic judgment, cite at least two independent sources from different categories (e.g., multilateral data + quality journalism, or think tank + primary national source). If only one source supports a claim, treat it as preliminary and say so in the prose.

---

## 4. False precision

**What it is.** Turning a range estimate into a point estimate, or attaching a number to something that is fundamentally a judgment. Saying "there is a 73% chance of election violence" when the analyst means "elevated risk." Or producing risk scores that look mathematical but are actually intuition with decimal points.

**Spot it.** Numbers without methodology. Risk scores like "4.7 out of 10." Probability statements without a basis for the specific number.

**Fix.** If using narrative analysis (which this skill defaults to), use calibrated language ("evidence points to," "trends suggest") and avoid pseudo-quantification. If using numeric risk scores, use a 1–5 scale only, define each level, and always pair the number with a one-sentence narrative justification.

---

## 5. Conclusion-first-then-evidence trap

**What it is.** The analyst settles on a view early — often based on initial impressions or prior familiarity — and then selects evidence that supports it. Contrary evidence gets minimized or excluded. This is confirmation bias in action.

**Spot it.** The brief has no surprises. Every section reinforces the same theme. Counter-evidence appears only in throwaway clauses ("though some observers disagree") without being engaged.

**Fix.** Before finalizing, explicitly ask: what would make my main judgment wrong? Surface that evidence and engage with it. The strongest briefs name the strongest counter-argument and explain why the analyst's reading is still more defensible.

---

## 6. Recency bias

**What it is.** Overweighting the past 30 days of headlines against a longer trajectory. A protest movement that surged in the last month feels existential; viewed against a 10-year political pattern, it may be cyclical.

**Spot it.** The brief leans heavily on events from the last quarter and treats them as decisive. The longer trend gets one paragraph; the recent event gets six.

**Fix.** For political and security analysis, ground each finding in at least a 3–5 year trajectory before bringing in recent events. The question to ask: does the recent event represent a structural break, or is it a continuation of an established pattern?

---

## 7. Ignoring sub-national variation

**What it is.** Treating a country as a single unit when meaningful variation exists across regions, urban/rural, ethnic, sectoral, or class lines. Saying "the security situation is deteriorating" when really it is deteriorating in three regions and stable in the rest.

**Spot it.** Sentences that say "the country is [adjective]" without further qualification. Maps absent from a brief about a country with active conflict.

**Fix.** For security, economic performance, humanitarian conditions, and infrastructure: name the sub-national variation explicitly. Use province/region-level data where available (ACLED is geocoded, IPC publishes sub-national maps, WB has sub-national poverty data for many countries).

---

## 8. Missing actor analysis

**What it is.** Describing what happened without naming who decided it and why. "Sanctions were tightened" — by whom, at whose initiative, against what objection? "The reform was passed" — whose reform, what coalition, what trade-offs?

**Spot it.** Passive-voice descriptions of major events. Government decisions presented as if they happen without agency.

**Fix.** For every significant event or decision in the brief, identify: who decided, who supported, who opposed, what alternative was available. This is what turns description into analysis.

---

## 9. Anchoring to a single narrative

**What it is.** The dominant narrative about a country — often shaped by major Western outlets or institutional consensus — becomes the brief's frame. Genuinely contested judgments get presented as settled.

**Spot it.** The brief sounds exactly like a recent FT or Economist piece on the country. Alternative readings present in regional press or local academia are absent.

**Fix.** Read at least one local-language or regional analytical source per brief, even if only for orientation. Where consensus exists, name it. Where the question is contested, name the contest.

---

## 10. Over-broad recommendations

**What it is.** Recommendations like "the country should improve governance" or "donors should coordinate better." Recommendations so generic they offer no decision support.

**Spot it.** Recommendations that could apply to any country at any time. Recommendations without an addressee or a specific action.

**Fix.** Every recommendation should have (a) an addressee, (b) a specific action, (c) a time horizon, and (d) a measurable indicator of progress. "Donors should coordinate" → "DAC donors operating in the health sector should consolidate sectoral planning under the existing Health Sector Coordinating Committee, with a joint annual review by [date]."

---

## 11. Cultural bias

**What it is.** Implicit Western liberal-democratic frameworks treated as universal standards, making the analysis less accurate for countries operating under different political logics. Examples include treating multi-party electoral competition as the only legitimate form of politics, or treating informal institutions as deviations from "real" institutions rather than as real institutions in their own right.

**Spot it.** Normatively loaded vocabulary ("corruption," "captured," "patronage") used without analyzing whether the practice is functionally analogous to formal institutions elsewhere. Surprise at outcomes that aren't actually surprising given local political logic.

**Fix.** Distinguish between analytical description and normative judgment. Where the analyst wants to make a normative point, label it as such; where the analyst is describing how the system actually works, do so without smuggling in the verdict.

---

## 12. Forecast as forecast-of-inevitability

**What it is.** Stating multi-month or multi-year forecasts in flat, declarative terms ("the regime will collapse," "the economy will recover") as if they were observed facts. Forecasts are judgments under uncertainty; the language should reflect that.

**Spot it.** Future-tense statements without conditional framing.

**Fix.** Use conditional framing: "on current trajectory, [X]"; "absent [specific change], [Y]"; "if [trigger event] occurs, [Z] becomes more likely." Name what would invalidate the forecast.

---

## 13. The "comprehensive" trap

**What it is.** Trying to cover every aspect of a country exhaustively, with the result that nothing gets emphasized. The reader leaves with no clear takeaway because the brief had no priorities.

**Spot it.** Each section is roughly the same length. No findings stand out. The executive summary has 12 bullets of equal weight.

**Fix.** Be ruthless about what's load-bearing for the user's decision and what's context. Section lengths should reflect importance for the brief's angle, not a notion of completeness. The executive summary should have 5–8 key judgments, not 15.

---

## Pre-delivery bias check

Before finalizing, run this self-check (it takes 5 minutes and catches most of the failures above):

1. Pick the brief's three most important judgments. For each: what specific evidence supports it? What evidence would contradict it? Is the counter-evidence in the brief?
2. Pick three statistics in the brief. Are they all dated within 3 years? Are sources cited?
3. Pick three recommendations. Does each have an addressee, a specific action, a time horizon, and a measurable indicator?
4. Read just the executive summary. Does it stand alone? Would a senior reader who only reads it know the bottom line?
5. Scan for passive-voice descriptions of major events. Rewrite to name who decided.
6. Scan for "the country is [adjective]" sentences. Add sub-national qualification where the data supports it.
7. Check the topic sentence of each paragraph. Is it analytic, or merely descriptive? Rewrite descriptive ones to lead with the judgment.

If the brief passes all seven, it is meaningfully better than 80% of country briefs in circulation.
