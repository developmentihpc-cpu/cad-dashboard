# Section Quality Reference

This reference codifies what separates strong analytical content from weak content for each section of a country brief. It is grounded in five country briefs produced during the skill's development (Ethiopia, Sudan, Syria, Morocco, Sri Lanka) and the specific analytical failures and successes observed across them.

The guidance is **prescriptive about analytical substance**, not formatting. The renderer handles formatting; this document handles whether what gets rendered is actually useful for a portfolio decision.

For each section, the format is:
- **Central question** the section must answer (the analytical anchor)
- **Signs of strong content**
- **Signs of weak content** (with specific failures observed)
- **Source priority** (which sources matter, in what order)
- **Data quality framing** (what to disclose about the analyst's confidence)
- **Common analytical traps** specific to this section

The fundamental principle running through every section: **describe-vs-analyze**. A description tells the reader what the institutional structure is. An analysis tells the reader what that structure implies for a portfolio decision. Strong sections analyze; weak sections describe.

A second fundamental principle: **each brief is standalone**. The reader has one brief in front of them — not the series. They do not know which other briefs exist; they have not read them; they should not need to acquire that context to understand this one. Phrases like "the most X of any country in this brief series" or "thinner than the UAE relationships covered in earlier briefs" leak the analyst's production context into the reader's experience and force the reader to wonder what they're missing. Describe the country on its own terms with absolute language ("the diaspora is among the most economically significant globally"; "the bilateral relationship is in an early formative phase"). The exception: explicit delta-summary blocks designed for repeat readers maintaining country watch lists, which exist precisely to compare against a prior version — there the comparison is the point.

## Length and decision-density discipline

Each section earns its space by decision-density, not by thoroughness. A 3-paragraph section that drives a portfolio decision is more valuable than an 8-paragraph section that reads as comprehensive but doesn't move the reader's mental model.

The rough length budget per section, calibrated against the May 2026 brief series (Ethiopia, Sudan, Syria, Morocco, Sri Lanka, Iran, Fiji):

| Section | Target length | Trim signal |
|---------|--------------|-------------|
| 1. Country Snapshot | ~8 indicators + 1-2 short paragraphs | Avoid recitation of facts a reader could Google |
| 1.5 Bilateral | Proportional to actual bilateral substance | Don't pad thin bilaterals; honest thinness is informative |
| 2. Political Context | 4-7 paragraphs + faction-box + leader-cards | This is high-density; full length justified |
| 3. Economic Conditions | 5-8 paragraphs + 1-2 charts | Full length when analysis is dense |
| 4. Security & Stability | 4-8 paragraphs + severity-box | Wartime briefs expand further |
| 5. Governance & Institutions | 2-4 paragraphs total | Trim if section reads as descriptive |
| 6. Social Dynamics | 2-3 paragraphs OR merge into other sections | Most countries don't need full section |
| Optional modules | Proportional to module relevance | Drop if marginal rather than padding |
| 17. Infrastructure | 1-3 paragraphs unless infrastructure-focused brief | Don't catalog all infrastructure |
| 18. Comparative | 1 table + 1-2 paragraphs | Cut country-by-country prose recitation |
| 19. Outlook | 3-5 scenario boxes + monitoring indicators | Full length always justified |
| Recommendations | 3-6 items with owner + timeline | Concrete > comprehensive |

**The "could this paragraph be deleted with no analytical loss?" test.** Apply at section-level: if you can delete a paragraph and the section's portfolio implications remain identical, delete the paragraph. The most common failure mode across the brief series has been Section 5 (Governance), Section 6 (Social), Section 17 (Infrastructure), and Section 18 (Comparative) running long with description-heavy content that doesn't change the reader's decision.

**When low-density sections should be cut entirely vs. compressed:**
- *Cut entirely* if the country's situation has no material dimension on that axis (e.g., Section 17 Infrastructure when the brief isn't infrastructure-focused and the country has no major pipeline)
- *Compress to 2-3 paragraphs* if the dimension matters but isn't the brief's central story (e.g., Section 5 Governance for a stable country)
- *Merge into adjacent section* if the content is genuinely relevant but doesn't merit its own section (e.g., Section 6 Social Dynamics merged into Section 2 Political Context when the social content IS the political content)

**The chart-decision-relevance test.** For every chart, ask: does this show trajectory, comparison, or composition that prose can't communicate efficiently? If the chart restates a single number that appears in the stats-strip, replace it with something that shows the dynamic. Charts that earn their space:
- Multi-period time series showing acceleration, deceleration, or inflection
- Cross-entity comparisons revealing disparities
- Stacked compositions revealing structural shifts
- Forward projections illustrating outlook claims

Charts that don't earn their space:
- Single-period bar charts of values already in prose
- Smooth monotonic series that look like a textbook example
- Charts where the chart title and the one analytical claim are the only signal

## Executive Summary

**Central question:** *"If the reader stops after the executive summary, what should they walk away with — and is it actually true?"*

The executive summary is the only section guaranteed to be read by senior decision-makers. Its function is not to summarize the rest of the brief; its function is to answer the portfolio decision question with appropriate calibration.

**Signs of strong content:**
- Opens with a one-sentence thesis that answers the analytical question the brief was commissioned to address. Not "Sri Lanka is recovering" but "Sri Lanka is investable with discipline."
- Verdict strip uses 4-5 dimensions chosen because they're decision-relevant (macro / security / governance / debt / climate), not because they're standard. The dimensions should change by country context.
- 4-7 key judgments, each with directional arrow (↑ improving / → stable / ↓ deteriorating / ⇅ contested) AND confidence level (high / moderate / low). The confidence levels should vary — if everything is "high confidence" the reader can't differentiate.
- Counterfactual block ("what would invalidate these judgments") that names *specific* events with *specific* triggers, not generic hedges.
- Risk matrix at 3-5 risks with calibrated likelihood and impact. If everything is "high likelihood, high impact" the matrix isn't doing work.
- Recommendations that are *actionable* — specifying owner, timeline, and decision implication. Not "engage carefully" but "engage in non-headline sectors; treat Adani disposition as the signal of NPP posture toward Indian capital."

**Signs of weak content (failures observed):**
- *Generic hedging*: "Sri Lanka faces risks but offers opportunities" — true of every country, useful for none.
- *Uniform confidence*: every judgment marked "moderate confidence" so the reader can't tell where the analyst has strong vs weak grounds.
- *Recommendations that re-describe the situation* rather than prescribing action: "Monitor the political situation closely" is not a recommendation, it's a restatement.
- *Missing counterfactual*: the brief asserts judgments without specifying what would change them, leaving the reader unable to update on new information.
- *Verdict strip dimensions that don't vary by context*: applying "macro / security / governance" to every country misses that Sudan needs "humanitarian / access / political settlement" while Morocco needs "macro / climate / political-space."

**Source priority:** The exec summary draws from everywhere — its sources are whatever the strongest evidence is for the specific claims being made. Citations in the exec summary should be among the most authoritative in the brief; this is not the section to cite Wikipedia or aggregator sources.

**Data quality framing:** The exec summary should signal uncertainty *in the prose*, not in a separate caveat. "Performance has outperformed program projections at every quarterly review" is high-confidence; "Performance trajectory appears positive but data quality is contested" is appropriately hedged. The hedging is invisible structural work, not explicit disclaimer.

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The "balanced view" trap*: pretending the brief has no analytical position by listing positives and negatives in equal weight. A brief without a thesis is not analysis.
- *The recommendation inflation trap*: producing 7-10 recommendations because more feels more thorough. 3-5 is usually right; more dilutes priority signal.
- *The headline-judgment trap*: leading with the most attention-grabbing judgment rather than the most decision-relevant. "Adani disputes test bilateral relations" is more dramatic but "Macro recovery is real but depends on three specific channels holding" is more useful.

## Section 1: Country Snapshot

**Central question:** *"What does an informed reader need to know about this country at the most basic level, AND what's changed recently that makes the country look different from its 5-year baseline?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Distinguishes baseline indicators (population, geography, religion — slow-moving) from stress indicators (active conflicts, debt distress, recent regime change — what makes today different). The visual layout should make this distinction explicit.
- "Stability" quadrant captures the *current state of stress*, not stable/unstable as a binary. Specific stressors: "100,000+ IDPs from Cyclone Ditwah"; "Three concurrent armed conflicts"; "Provincial elections deferred 18 months past constitutional date."
- Names specific leaders (President, PM) with tenure dates — these are who decision-relevant correspondence will reference.
- 4 quadrants × 4-6 items each. More becomes a wall of facts; fewer becomes superficial.
- Includes a "last reviewed" date so readers know if the snapshot is current.

**Signs of weak content (failures observed):**
- *Mixed weighting*: "Population ~120 million" and "Three concurrent armed conflicts" listed at the same visual weight, with no signal which matters analytically. (Ethiopia brief.)
- *Baseline-only*: indicators that haven't changed in 10 years dominate, with no current-stress dimension. Reader can't tell why this brief was commissioned now.
- *Quantitative fragmentation*: lots of specific figures without one synthesizing observation. Reader has data but no narrative.
- *Sub-national flattening*: a country with significant sub-national variation gets one nationally-averaged snapshot. (Sudan should signal RSF-vs-SAF in the snapshot itself.)

**Source priority:**
1. Most recent IMF Article IV for macro figures (independently validated, methodologically consistent).
2. Country central bank monthly bulletin for most current data (typically 1-2 months ahead of IMF cycle).
3. Department of Statistics / Census Bureau for demographic baseline.
4. OCHA Situation Report for IDP/humanitarian figures.
5. Election Commission for most recent election results.

**Data quality framing:** A single sentence after the snapshot grid addressing: vintage (how recent), confidence (whether stats apparatus functions), and coverage (which parts of the country are uncovered).

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The "stable" trap*: a country with no active armed conflict gets labeled "stable" — but stability is multi-dimensional. Morocco has no armed conflict but has political-space concerns. Don't use single-word stability labels.
- *The leadership-list trap*: listing cabinets exhaustively. 3-4 most decision-relevant figures are enough; full cabinets belong in political context.
- *The constitutional-perfection trap*: citing what the constitution says when the operational reality differs.

## Section 1.5: Bilateral Relations

**Central question:** *"What is the binding context of the home country's relationship with this target country — and how does that relationship constrain or enable engagement?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Names the home country explicitly in the section heading (e.g., "Bilateral Relations: India" not just "Bilateral Relations"). The 1.5 numbering signals this is conditional content.
- Bilateral-stats strip with 4 tiles capturing the most decision-relevant numbers: bilateral trade, cumulative FDI, diaspora population, aid disbursement. Numbers should be from the most recent fiscal year and sourced to authoritative bilateral databases (Ministry of Commerce, Embassy, central bank).
- Decision implication callout that specifically translates the bilateral context into operational guidance: what does this relationship mean for project decisions in this target country?
- Distinguishes pre-crisis baseline from post-crisis acceleration when relevant. India-Sri Lanka relations look fundamentally different post-2022 than pre-2022.
- Senior visits and engagements over an 18-24 month window, with specific dates and themes — not "Both sides exchanged visits."
- Treaties table with status (in force / negotiating / suspended) — not just a list of agreement names.
- Honest treatment of contested elements (Adani disputes for India-Sri Lanka; sanctions exposure for UAE-Russia bilateral).

**Signs of weak content:**
- *Generic relationship-building language*: "The two countries enjoy warm and growing relations." Diplomatic boilerplate, no analytical content.
- *Aggregate trade figure without composition*: "$5.97B in bilateral trade" without saying who exports what, in what trend direction, with what concentration risk.
- *Investment list rather than analysis*: naming every Indian company present in Sri Lanka rather than analyzing what sectors matter.
- *Missing political-economy*: treating the bilateral relationship as purely economic when the political dimension is binding (India's strategic interest in countering Chinese influence is more important than the trade figure for portfolio decisions).
- *Absence of friction acknowledgment*: presenting bilateral relations as universally positive when there are real disputes (Adani, ethnic-Tamil dimension, fisheries disputes).

**Source priority:**
1. Home country foreign ministry's bilateral relations brief (most authoritative).
2. Embassy / High Commission economic and trade engagement page.
3. Target country's Board of Investment or equivalent.
4. Recent Joint Commission / Joint Economic Committee meeting outcomes.
5. Bilateral trade data from home country's Directorate General of Commercial Intelligence and Statistics (or equivalent).
6. Independent analysis (ORF, ICWA, regional think tanks) for political-economy framing.

**Data quality framing:** Bilateral trade figures can vary by ~10-20% between source countries' reporting (different fiscal years, different inclusion of services, different valuation methods). Note which side's figures you're citing. FDI figures are particularly unreliable due to round-tripping through Mauritius / Singapore / Cyprus.

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The Wikipedia trap*: citing "deep historical and cultural ties dating back 2,500 years" filler that adds words but no analytical signal.
- *The bilateral-isolation trap*: analyzing the bilateral relationship without referencing the broader strategic context (India-Sri Lanka relations matter partly because of China; UAE-Sudan relations matter partly because of Saudi-Egypt rivalry).
- *The diplomatic-rhetoric trap*: citing official joint statements as analytical content. Joint statements signal direction but contain no analytical depth.
- *The cumulative-FDI trap*: citing cumulative FDI since some baseline year that includes long-since-divested investments. Current-stock figures are usually more analytically useful.

**When to omit Section 1.5:** If the home country has no meaningful bilateral relationship with the target country (e.g., UAE-Bolivia, or a UAE brief on a Pacific island state), the section should be omitted with `--home-country none` rather than padded with thin content.

## Section 2: Political Context

**Central question:** *"Who actually decides, and what happens when their mandate erodes?"*

The Political Context section is the longest narrative section in the first half of the brief because politics drives most other dimensions. Weak political-context analysis tends to describe institutional structures; strong political-context analysis identifies the *binding constraints* on policy and the *trajectory variables* that will determine the next 18-24 months.

**The formal-vs-operational power question.** Every Section 2 must explicitly answer: *is the constitutional commander-in-chief the operational decision-maker, or is real power held elsewhere?* This is the single most important analytical question for the section and it's also the one most commonly skipped. The candidate answers a brief should evaluate:

- *Convergent (formal = operational power):* the constitutional power-holder is the actual decision-maker. Sri Lanka under AKD with the NPP supermajority is a clean case — formal and operational power converge in the presidency. Most consolidated democracies fit here.
- *Military as parallel state:* the armed forces operate as an independent political-economic actor, often controlling key portfolios (foreign policy, security, large sectors of the economy) regardless of which civilians hold formal office. Pakistan (army's enduring control of foreign and security policy), Egypt (military's economic empire under Sisi), Algeria (*le pouvoir*), pre-2025 Sudan (SAF as institution), Myanmar (military council in formal control post-2021 coup).
- *Religious authority as parallel structure:* clerical bodies hold independent decision-making power that constrains or supersedes civilian government. Iran (Supreme Leader + Assembly of Experts above the elected presidency), Saudi Arabia (the religious establishment's traditional role, though attenuated under MBS).
- *Monarchic supremacy:* formal civilian government operates under explicit monarchic authority. Morocco (the King's executive authority over the constitution), Jordan, Gulf monarchies in varying degrees.
- *Oligarchic networks:* economic-elite networks hold de facto decision-making power independent of formal office (some Central Asian states; Ukraine pre-2014 in certain dimensions).
- *External actor primacy:* an external state's preferences are the binding constraint on key decisions (post-1990 Lebanon under Syrian and later Iranian influence; Bosnia under the High Representative; some post-conflict transition periods).
- *Contested authority:* multiple actors claim legitimate authority and the answer is unresolved. Post-coup transition periods; civil-war states with competing governments.

A brief that doesn't address this question — or that treats the constitutional structure as if it were the operational reality — has a visible analytical gap. The faction-box should reflect the answer: if the military is the parallel power, it appears in the faction-box with the `military` category. If religious authority is, it appears with `religious-authority`. Leader-cards in Section 2 should include the actual decision-makers, not only the constitutional figureheads — the Pakistan Army Chief belongs in the cards alongside the Prime Minister; Iran's Supreme Leader alongside the President.

**Signs of strong content:**
- Anchors on a central political question (what is the durable mandate? what is the binding constraint on reform? what is the succession risk?) rather than describing the institutional structure.
- Faction box that genuinely identifies the 3-5 most decision-relevant political actors with their positions on dimensions of brief interest. Includes external actors (Eritrea in Ethiopia; Israel and Iran for Syria) when they're operationally relevant. **Includes the military as a faction (`military` category) when armed forces are an independent political-economic actor, not just an institution under civilian control.**
- Distinguishes *formal* from *operational* power structure explicitly. Sri Lanka's NPP supermajority makes formal-vs-operational distinction collapse; Syria's transitional government has wide formal authority but contested operational reach; Pakistan's civilian government holds formal authority that the military substantively constrains in foreign and security domains.
- **Leader-cards in this section include de facto power-holders, not just the constitutional officeholders.** For a military-primacy state, the Army Chief is in the cards. For Iran, the Supreme Leader is in the cards. For Morocco, the King is in the cards alongside the Prime Minister. The selection signals the analysis.
- Names the political-trajectory variable explicitly: "Will the NPP government deliver provincial council elections in 2026?" This becomes the indicator a reader can track.
- Treats civil-military relations as a distinct dimension where relevant — most countries this brief series covers have civil-military as a binding constraint.

**Signs of weak content (failures observed):**
- *Institutional description without analytical conclusion*: "Sri Lanka is a semi-presidential republic with 9 provincial councils. The president has executive authority. Parliament has 225 seats." All true, none analytically useful. (Sri Lanka brief partially fell into this.)
- *Missing the binding constraint*: treating political dynamics as freely variable when there are structural constraints (Morocco's monarchic supremacy; Ethiopia's federal-state ethnic structure; UAE's federal structure).
- *Treating the constitutional structure as if it were the operational reality* in countries where formal and de facto power diverge. Writing about Pakistan as if the Prime Minister is the decision-maker on India policy; writing about Egypt as if civilian institutions constrain Sisi the way they would constrain a democratically-elected leader; writing about Iran as if the elected presidency is the apex of authority. Each of these is a substantial analytical error that the formal-vs-operational question would have caught.
- *Bias toward English-language sources on local politics*: relying on international media analysis when local-language political reporting captures dynamics that Western sources miss.
- *Personality-driven without institutional grounding*: focusing on leaders' personalities without explaining the institutional context that constrains them.
- *Election-only frame*: treating political context as electoral dynamics, missing that elections may not be the most decision-relevant variable.

**Source priority:**
1. International Crisis Group country reports (highest analytical quality on political dynamics).
2. The country's most credible domestic independent analyst (Ground Views for Sri Lanka; Mada Masr for Egypt; Daraj for Lebanon).
3. Regional think tanks (ORF for South Asia; ECFR for MENA; ISS for Africa).
4. BTI country report (every 2 years, comprehensive governance baseline).
5. Embassy political reporting (where accessible).
6. *Avoid* unreliable: aggregator sites, UPSC notes, generic country profiles that recycle dated material.

**Data quality framing:** Political analysis is inherently interpretive. Distinguish *fact* (election results, cabinet composition, legislation passed) from *interpretation* (what coalition stability implies, what reform-momentum signals). Strong sections do this implicitly through verb choice ("the government declared" vs "the government appears to have signaled").

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The constitutional-formalist trap*: treating constitutional structure as governance reality. Most countries' constitutional structures differ from operational governance.
- *The Western-democracy-projection trap*: evaluating non-Western political systems against Western democratic benchmarks. The relevant question is what the system actually does, not whether it matches a benchmark.
- *The election-centrism trap*: treating elections as the central political variable when in many systems (UAE, Saudi Arabia, China, post-Assad Syria) the operational political dynamics are non-electoral.
- *The "stable authoritarianism" trap*: assuming authoritarian systems are stable because they don't show electoral volatility. They're often more brittle precisely because they don't have electoral pressure-release valves.

## Section 3: Economic Conditions

**Central question:** *"Is the macroeconomic trajectory sustainable, and what specifically would derail it?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Opens with a sentence positioning the current macroeconomic state relative to a baseline (pre-crisis, pre-default, pre-reform). The reader needs the trajectory, not the snapshot.
- Walks through the principal macro dimensions in order: growth, inflation, external balance, fiscal balance, sectoral composition. Each gets a paragraph with the relevant figures and the analytical takeaway.
- Sub-national variation paragraph when it's analytically relevant (Sri Lanka's Western Province 39% of GDP; Ethiopia's Amhara/Oromia economic stress).
- Cites specific figures from authoritative sources with vintage. "March 2026 inflation: 2.2% y/y per CBSL" — not "Inflation is low."
- Connects macro figures to drivers: growth from services concentration; reserves rebuilding from remittance surge; fiscal performance from vehicle import revenues (one-time effect).
- Includes a chart when one helps analytical clarity (typically GDP growth + inflation over 5-7 years, or debt service profile).

**Signs of weak content:**
- *Indicator-list-without-narrative*: "GDP growth was 5%. Inflation was 2.2%. Reserves are $7B. Debt is 100% of GDP." Numbers without synthesis.
- *Missing data-quality flag*: citing specific figures for countries where the statistical apparatus is compromised (Sudan, Syria pre-2025, Lebanon) without flagging that confidence intervals are wide.
- *Sectoral-composition list*: "Services 58%, Industry 30%, Agriculture 8%" without explaining what that composition implies for vulnerability or growth path.
- *Pre-crisis comparison missing*: showing 2025 figures without anchoring them to pre-2022 baseline. "5% growth" looks different against "Sri Lanka contracted 7.3% in 2022."
- *Inflation-without-distribution*: citing headline inflation without addressing whether it's broad-based or driven by specific categories (food inflation hits poor households differently than core inflation).

**Source priority:**
1. Most recent IMF Article IV consultation report.
2. Country central bank monetary policy reports and monthly bulletins.
3. Country Ministry of Finance / Treasury budget documents.
4. World Bank Macro-Poverty Outlook (semi-annual, country chapters).
5. ADB / AfDB country regional outlooks where relevant.
6. *Avoid*: TradingEconomics aggregator (lagging and sometimes wrong); generic Wikipedia economy pages (often years out of date).

**Data quality framing:** Macro data quality varies dramatically. Sri Lanka's CBSL produces near-real-time, internationally-validated data. Sudan's macro figures are largely IMF-staff estimates with substantial uncertainty. Syria's are being reconstituted. Make the quality assessment explicit: "Sri Lanka maintains a high-quality statistical apparatus; figures here can be treated with confidence" or "Sudan's macro figures are best-guess estimates with significant uncertainty given conflict-driven coverage gaps."

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The recovery-narrative trap*: treating two years of post-crisis growth as evidence of durable recovery without examining the drivers (Sri Lanka 2024-25 growth was concentrated in services + construction + one-time vehicle import revenues).
- *The headline-figure trap*: anchoring analysis on the headline GDP growth or inflation number without addressing what's underneath.
- *The "fundamentals" hand-wave*: using the word "fundamentals" without specifying which fundamentals are strong vs weak.
- *The IMF-projection-as-fact trap*: citing IMF projections (which are baseline scenarios contingent on policy adherence) as if they were forecasts.

## Section 4: Security & Stability

**Central question:** *"What does the security environment mean operationally for someone working in this country, and what's the most likely trajectory over the next 18-24 months?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Distinguishes types of security risk: armed conflict, civil unrest, terrorism, crime, security-services overreach. Each has different operational implications.
- Sub-national severity map (`::: map` choropleth + `::: severity-box` table) when sub-national variation is the analytically central story (Sudan, Ethiopia, Syria). Reference map only when security is relatively uniform (Sri Lanka, Morocco).
- Severity categories connect to operational implications: what does a "high" severity region mean for personnel safety, asset risk, project completion, insurance availability?
- External security dimensions: relations with neighbors, alliance structures, force posture changes.
- Specific recent flashpoints with dates and sources, not generic descriptions.
- ACLED political-violence event count (annual chart) when conflict intensity is material.

**Signs of weak content (failures observed):**
- *Color-coded geography without operational translation*: producing a severity map with five categories without explaining what each category means for someone working there. (Syria brief — my own failure.)
- *Description without trajectory*: telling the reader the security situation today without indicating whether it's improving, stable, or deteriorating.
- *Missing security-services dimension*: focusing on armed-actor risk while ignoring that the security services themselves can be a risk vector (PTA in Sri Lanka; emergency law in Egypt; mukhabarat in pre-2024 Syria).
- *Single-author depth limit*: the analyst is not a security specialist (this is true of my five briefs); the section reflects available open-source intelligence rather than field knowledge.

**Source priority:**
1. ACLED (Armed Conflict Location and Event Data) for political violence event data — single most useful structured source.
2. International Crisis Group country reports for conflict analysis.
3. UN Security Council reports (for UN-mandated peacekeeping countries).
4. ECHO (EU Civil Protection and Humanitarian Aid) for humanitarian-conflict overlap.
5. ISS (Institute for Security Studies, Africa) for African conflicts; IISS Military Balance for force posture.
6. US Department of State Travel Advisory (a useful sub-national risk signal even if politically calibrated).

**Data quality framing:** Conflict data is notoriously contested. ACLED is the most rigorous open-source dataset but undercounts in inaccessible areas (RSF-held Darfur; pre-2025 northwest Syria). Note coverage limitations explicitly. Casualty figures from one side of a conflict should be treated with skepticism.

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The "no active conflict means stable" trap*: post-conflict countries (Syria post-Assad, Sri Lanka post-2009) can be operationally non-stable without active conflict (security services overreach, occupied territory disputes, latent insurgency).
- *The terrorism-overweighting trap*: a single high-profile attack (Easter Sunday Sri Lanka 2019) can warp risk assessment relative to chronic but lower-profile risks (police brutality, criminal violence, traffic accidents).
- *The "stability = predictability" trap*: stable-looking situations (Sudan pre-April 2023; Ukraine pre-February 2022) can transition rapidly to high-violence. Stability is not predictability.
- *The "capacity = intent" trap*: treating armed actors' capability as evidence of intent to use it. The TDF retains heavy weapons; whether they intend to use them is a separate question.

## Section 5: Governance & Rule of Law

**Central question:** *"What's the trajectory of institutional quality, and what specifically would change it over the next 18-24 months?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Distinguishes *stock* (institutional baseline, slow-moving) from *flow* (recent changes, fast-moving). A reader needs both.
- Names specific institutions by their actual names (CIABOC for Sri Lanka's anti-corruption commission; Egypt's Administrative Control Authority) — not generic categories.
- Tracks specific governance variables: anti-corruption capacity, judicial independence, electoral integrity, press freedom, civil society space. Each is distinct.
- Connects governance trajectory to portfolio implications: governance reform supports SOE engagement; governance backsliding signals investment risk; press freedom indicates whether civil society partners can operate.
- Addresses contradictions: a government can simultaneously strengthen anti-corruption (CIABOC reactivation) and weaken civil society space (Online Safety Act).

**Signs of weak content (failures observed):**
- *Stock-only*: citing Worldwide Governance Indicators or BTI scores without addressing what's changed in the last 18-24 months. (Most of my briefs lean this way.)
- *Index-soup*: stringing together rankings (WGI, V-Dem, BTI, Press Freedom Index, Corruption Perceptions Index) without analytical synthesis. The rankings differ in methodology; their conjunction isn't more useful than the strongest single one.
- *Western-democracy-projection*: scoring governance against Western institutional benchmarks. The relevant question is what the system actually does, not whether it matches a benchmark.
- *Reform-promise inventory*: listing all the reforms the government has promised without examining which have been implemented.

**Source priority:**
1. World Justice Project Rule of Law Index (most rigorous methodology).
2. Bertelsmann Transformation Index (BTI) — comprehensive biennial assessment.
3. Worldwide Governance Indicators (WGI) — useful for trajectory but lagging.
4. V-Dem (Varieties of Democracy) for liberal democracy components.
5. UNDP-OECD country governance assessments where available.
6. Reporters Without Borders Press Freedom Index and CPJ for press dimension.
7. Transparency International CPI for corruption-perception (but note: perception, not measurement).
8. UN Human Rights Council UPR cycle reports.

**Data quality framing:** Governance indices lag by 1-2 years. They capture stock well but trajectory weakly. Recent press freedom rulings, recent court decisions, recent civil society NGO restrictions are not yet reflected. Strong governance sections cite indices for baseline and recent news for trajectory.

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The "improving" trap*: a government that has reformed anti-corruption procedures but continued PTA use is doing both — call it both.
- *The election-equals-democracy trap*: free elections do not equal liberal democracy. Sri Lanka has free elections AND uses PTA; Morocco has elections AND has reduced political-space.
- *The "rule of law" semantic trap*: rule of law has thick (legal-philosophical) and thin (procedural) definitions. Be explicit about which you mean.
- *The Worldwide-Governance-Indicators-trap*: WGI percentiles are highly correlated with GDP per capita — they may capture income more than governance. Use cautiously.

## Section 6: Social & Human Development

**Central question:** *"What does the social/human development trajectory imply for medium-term political stability and growth quality?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Anchored in HDI position relative to GDP per capita (Sri Lanka punches above its income on HDI; Pakistan punches below). This is the diagnostic.
- Health, education, poverty, labor market, gender — each gets a paragraph with specific indicators and trajectory.
- Sub-national variation when material (Northern Province Sri Lanka; Sahel Morocco; Tigray Ethiopia post-conflict humanitarian impact).
- Demographic dimension (Sri Lanka's earlier-than-peers demographic transition; Egypt's youth bulge; UAE's expatriate-dominated demographics) when it shapes the medium-term outlook.
- Brain drain / labor migration linked to remittance section.

**Signs of weak content:**
- *MDG/SDG-status recitation*: "On track for X, off track for Y" without analytical implication.
- *Health-system description without stress test*: medical apparatus described without addressing how it performed during 2022-23 crisis or COVID.
- *Education quality dismissed*: treating high literacy / enrollment as equivalent to high education quality (Sri Lanka is highly literate; education quality and labor-market alignment are separately problematic).
- *Gender as compliance check*: female labor force participation cited as a number without analytical context (Sri Lanka's 32% FLFP is structurally below what education indicators predict — that's the analytical observation, not the number).

**Source priority:**
1. UNDP Human Development Report (annual; HDI + sub-indices).
2. World Bank Poverty and Equity Briefs (country-specific, recent).
3. UNICEF, WHO country statistical profiles.
4. UNESCO Institute for Statistics for education data.
5. ILOSTAT for labor force participation and unemployment.
6. UNFPA / UN DESA for demographic projections.

**Data quality framing:** Social development data lags significantly — most figures are 2-3 years behind. Poverty figures are particularly contested (different poverty lines, different national vs international comparisons). Note vintage and methodology.

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The literacy-equals-skills trap*: high literacy rates do not imply high skill levels or labor-market readiness.
- *The HDI-overaggregation trap*: HDI hides sub-national variation; the national figure can look good while substantial populations are in worse-than-HDI conditions.
- *The "demographic dividend" trap*: a youth bulge is a dividend only if jobs are created; otherwise it's a destabilizer.
- *The gender-compliance trap*: treating gender as a box-check (female literacy, female labor force participation) rather than asking what gender dynamics imply for political and economic trajectories.

## Section 7: Development Project Context

**Central question:** *"What is the specific development project that's the analytical center of gravity, and how does the country context shape its prospects?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Names the specific project (or project portfolio) with size, sector, counterparts, and timeline.
- Identifies the institutional partners on both sides — who in the country government is responsible, who in the home-country institution.
- Connects the project's success conditions to the broader country analysis: which sections of the brief most directly affect the project's prospects.
- Identifies specific risks to the project from the country context (political continuity, regulatory stability, security, currency).
- Treats the development-project section as connecting the country analysis to the operational decision the brief is supporting.

**Signs of weak content:**
- *Generic "development cooperation" framing* without naming a specific project.
- *Project description without country-context translation*: telling the reader about the project but not connecting it to the country dynamics analyzed elsewhere in the brief.
- *Risk-listing without prioritization*: enumerating all conceivable risks without identifying which 2-3 are binding.

**Source priority:**
1. Home country institution's internal project documents (where accessible).
2. Counterpart government ministry documents.
3. Multilateral co-financing documents (World Bank, ADB Project Appraisal Documents).
4. Recent independent reviews (DEval, IEG, ICR).

**Data quality framing:** Internal project documents are higher quality than public-facing materials but may have selection bias toward positive framing. External evaluations are more candid.

**When to include Section 7:** Only when there's a specific project anchoring the brief. For exploratory briefs without a project anchor, omit this section.

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The promotion-doc trap*: project briefs that are essentially marketing for an existing project, without genuine risk analysis.
- *The risk-symmetry trap*: treating all identified risks as equally weighted; in practice 1-2 risks usually dominate.
- *The "implementation will be challenging" trap*: hedging without specifying which specific implementation factors are challenging.

## Section 8: Humanitarian Severity

**Central question:** *"What is the scale and trajectory of humanitarian need, and what does response capacity look like?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Distinguishes acute humanitarian crisis (Sudan: 25.6M in need) from discrete shock (Sri Lanka Ditwah: 100,000 displaced, $1.62B reconstruction). Different analytical frames.
- Uses IPC (Integrated Food Security Phase Classification) categories where relevant.
- Specific recent figures from OCHA Situation Reports with dates.
- Response architecture: who is funding, who is implementing, what the funding gap is.
- Access dimension: which areas are operationally accessible, what restrictions apply.
- Sub-national variation as central, not afterthought.

**Signs of weak content:**
- *Generic humanitarian-language*: "humanitarian needs remain significant" without specifying what kind of need at what scale.
- *Capacity-without-funding*: describing humanitarian organizations present without addressing whether they're funded.
- *Aggregate-only*: national-level IDP figures without sub-national distribution.
- *Missing access dimension*: treating humanitarian operations as if access were uniform when access varies dramatically by armed-actor control.

**Source priority:**
1. UN OCHA Humanitarian Needs Overview (annual) and Situation Reports (weekly/monthly).
2. UN OCHA Financial Tracking Service (FTS) for funding data.
3. ECHO Crisis Reports for EU humanitarian assessment.
4. Specific cluster reports (Food Security Cluster, Protection Cluster, WASH Cluster).
5. ICRC and MSF assessments where available.
6. IPC Food Insecurity classification reports.

**Data quality framing:** Humanitarian data quality is heavily constrained by access. RSF-held Darfur has limited assessment coverage; northeast Syria pre-SDF integration had limited UN access. Figures are typically the lower bound of actual need.

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The numbers-fatigue trap*: very large numbers ($X billion in need, Y million people affected) lose impact through familiarity. Specific, vivid examples retain analytical weight.
- *The discrete-vs-chronic confusion trap*: treating discrete shocks (cyclone, earthquake) the same as chronic crisis (Sudan civil war). Different recovery pathways, different financing needs.
- *The "international community" trap*: invoking abstract international response without specifying which donors are funding which gaps.
- *The funding-gap trap*: citing the funding gap without addressing whether the appeal itself is realistic or aspirational.

## Section 9: Macro Stress & Debt Sustainability Deep-Dive

**Central question:** *"Is the macroeconomic trajectory sustainable on current trajectory, and what specifically would derail it?"*

This is one of the strongest sections in my 5 briefs because it forces analytical discipline: a clear central question, explicit data, traceable causation. Use it as the template for what every section aspires to.

**Signs of strong content:**
- Opens with positioning relative to a baseline (pre-default, pre-crisis, pre-program).
- Walks through debt structure: total external debt, multilateral vs bilateral vs commercial composition, restructuring status.
- Debt service profile chart showing maturities over 5-7 years. The shape matters — front-loaded vs back-loaded debt has different implications.
- Vulnerability assessment with 3-4 specific transmission channels and their magnitude.
- Cost-recovery or other structural-benchmark analysis when relevant to IMF program continuity.
- Scenarios (base/plausible/downside) with explicit probabilities and drivers.

**Signs of weak content:**
- *Debt-stock without structure*: citing total external debt without composition.
- *Debt-service-cliff missed*: not addressing when external debt service obligations spike (Sri Lanka 2028-29 is the moment of stress).
- *Generic "vulnerability"*: citing macroeconomic vulnerability without identifying which transmission channels are most relevant.
- *IMF-program-as-faith*: treating IMF program continuation as automatic; programs go off-track.

**Source priority:**
1. Most recent IMF Country Report (Article IV or program review).
2. IMF Debt Sustainability Analysis (often in IMF Country Reports).
3. Country Treasury Investor Presentations (for sovereign debt structure).
4. Rating agency reports (Fitch, S&P, Moody's) for credit market perspective.
5. Central bank balance of payments / external sector data.
6. Sovereign debt restructuring updates (G20 Common Framework documentation; Paris Club).

**Data quality framing:** Sovereign debt figures from IMF Country Reports are well-validated. Debt restructuring details from Treasury Investor Presentations are typically authoritative because they're targeted at creditor relations. Be careful with rating-agency analysis — useful for market perception, but rating-agency analysis sometimes lags actual developments.

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The post-program graduation trap*: assuming a country graduates from IMF programs successfully when historical base rates suggest many countries return for follow-on programs (Pakistan, Argentina, Egypt).
- *The "debt is sustainable" trap*: sustainability assessments depend on specific assumptions; the IMF's baseline is one scenario, not a guarantee.
- *The restructuring-completion trap*: treating "99% of creditors agreed" as "restructuring complete" when 1% holdouts (especially commercial) can litigate and create disruption.
- *The reserve-adequacy trap*: citing reserves in months of imports without addressing what's driving the cover (e.g., import-compression-driven cover declines when imports normalize).

## Section 10: Climate Risk

**Central question:** *"How does climate risk shape this country's economic and humanitarian trajectory, and what does that imply for portfolio time horizons?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Distinguishes physical risk (cyclones, drought, sea-level rise, water stress) from transition risk (carbon-intensive sectors facing policy or market shifts).
- Specific recent events with damage figures (Sri Lanka Ditwah; Morocco drought; Ethiopia recurring drought-flood cycles).
- Sectoral exposure: agriculture's share of GDP and labor; coastal infrastructure exposure; water-intensive industries.
- Adaptation capacity dimension: what the country can absorb, what overwhelms its capacity.
- Transition opportunities (renewables potential, green investment frameworks) where they're meaningfully present.
- Climate-economic linkages: drought → agricultural output → rural employment → migration.

**Signs of weak content:**
- *Generic climate-vulnerable framing*: "the country is vulnerable to climate change" without specific exposure analysis.
- *Adaptation-without-economics*: discussing adaptation in isolation from financing capacity.
- *Transition risk skipped*: focusing entirely on physical risk while ignoring transition risk for hydrocarbon-dependent economies.
- *Aggregate climate scores*: citing ND-GAIN or similar without addressing what the score actually means analytically.

**Source priority:**
1. World Bank Country Climate and Development Reports (CCDRs) — most comprehensive country-level synthesis.
2. IPCC regional chapters for physical-risk projections.
3. UNFCCC Nationally Determined Contributions (NDC) for transition commitments.
4. National meteorological / disaster management agencies for historical event data.
5. Reinsurer reports (Munich Re, Swiss Re) for event cost estimates.

**Data quality framing:** Climate projections involve substantial uncertainty bands; cite ranges, not point estimates. Historical event costs are better established than future projections.

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The future-discounting trap*: climate risks discounted because they're long-horizon, when in fact recent events (Ditwah, Pakistan floods 2022, Morocco drought) show shorter time horizons.
- *The adaptation-as-solution trap*: assuming adaptation finance closes the climate vulnerability gap when funding falls well short of needs.
- *The transition-as-developed-country-issue trap*: treating transition risk as relevant only to OECD economies, missing that frontier-market hydrocarbon exporters face transition risk.

---

## Section 11: Election Cycle & Political Calendar

**Central question:** *"What is the most likely electoral outcome in the relevant window, and what does it imply for policy continuity that makes today's analysis still relevant 18 months from now?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Lists upcoming elections (presidential, parliamentary, sub-national) with specific dates.
- For each, identifies the likely outcome and the probability range.
- Connects electoral outcomes to policy variables: which policies are sticky regardless of outcome; which depend on specific outcomes.
- Treats *not* holding elections (deferral) as a political variable in itself.
- For non-electoral systems (UAE, Saudi Arabia), substitutes succession dynamics, royal family dynamics, or other relevant decision processes.

**Signs of weak content:**
- *Election-date-without-prediction*: citing "next elections due 2029" without analytical prediction.
- *Generic "uncertainty around elections"*: hedging without committing to a most-likely-outcome assessment.
- *Missing sub-national*: focusing on presidential/parliamentary while ignoring locally relevant elections.
- *Treating election deferral as procedural*: when in many contexts deferral is itself the political variable (Sri Lanka provincial councils).

**Source priority:**
1. Election Commission of the country.
2. International Foundation for Electoral Systems (IFES) country reports.
3. Polling data where reliable (Sri Lanka has reasonable polling; many countries don't).
4. Recent election results from credible observation missions (EU EOM, OSCE/ODIHR, Commonwealth).
5. Domestic political analysis for prediction.

**Data quality framing:** Pre-election polling in many countries is unreliable. Cite polls with methodology caveats.

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The "fair elections = good outcome" trap*: free elections can produce outcomes that worsen governance (Hungary post-2010, Venezuela 1998).
- *The status-quo-bias trap*: assuming incumbents win because they usually do, missing inflection points (Sri Lanka 2024 election was the inflection point most analysts under-weighted).
- *The polling-as-fact trap*: treating pre-election polling as data when it's often loosely sourced and politically calibrated.

## Section 12: Civil Society, Media & Reconciliation

**Central question:** *"What is the operating space for civil society, independent media, and reconciliation processes, and what does that imply for engagement with non-state actors?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Geographic specificity about civil society space (Colombo vs Northern Province in Sri Lanka; Khartoum vs Darfur in Sudan).
- Specific legislation and its enforcement (PTA in Sri Lanka, Online Safety Act, NGO registration requirements).
- Press freedom dimension with specific recent events (closure of outlet X, prosecution of journalist Y).
- Reconciliation dimension for post-conflict countries — substantive, not symbolic.
- Treats civil society and media as related but distinct dimensions.

**Signs of weak content:**
- *Generic civil-society-space-language*: "civil society faces challenges" without specifics.
- *Reconciliation-as-rhetoric*: focusing on reconciliation rhetoric rather than substantive action.
- *Press freedom rankings without trajectory*: citing rank without addressing recent press-freedom incidents.
- *Missing legal-instrument detail*: treating restrictive laws as generic when they're specific tools with specific application patterns.

**Source priority:**
1. CIVICUS Monitor for civil society space ratings.
2. Reporters Without Borders Press Freedom Index and CPJ for press dimension.
3. International Crisis Group for reconciliation analysis.
4. UN Human Rights Council UPR cycle reports.
5. Domestic civil society organizations directly (Ground Views, Mada Masr, etc.).

**Data quality framing:** Civil society space and media freedom assessments are necessarily perception-based; methodology matters. Cross-reference at least two sources for major claims.

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The "improving" trap*: civil society space can improve in some dimensions (less violent crackdown) while worsening in others (more sophisticated surveillance).
- *The reconciliation-process trap*: process commitments (TRC established, dialogue initiated) ≠ substantive reconciliation outcomes.
- *The Western-NGO-centric trap*: focusing on international NGOs' operating space while ignoring domestic civil society's experience.

## Section 13: Diaspora & Remittances

**Central question:** *"What does the diaspora structure mean for external balance stability, return-investment potential, and political dynamics?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Distinguishes major diaspora populations: economic migrants vs political refugees vs historic communities; skilled vs unskilled labor.
- Remittance flows by source country with trajectory (sticky Gulf flows vs growing skilled-migrant flows).
- Diaspora political engagement: which diaspora populations are politically active and on which issues.
- Gendered dimension: female labor migration patterns and their distinct protections.
- Return migration: incentives, capacity, and timing.

**Signs of weak content:**
- *Aggregate-diaspora treatment*: treating diaspora as a single category when it's distinct populations.
- *Remittance-without-vulnerability*: citing remittance volumes without addressing source-country concentration risk.
- *Missing political dimension*: economic-only diaspora analysis when diaspora politics shape home-country dynamics (Tamil diaspora for Sri Lanka; Ethiopian diaspora for the Tigray conflict).

**Source priority:**
1. World Bank Migration and Remittances data.
2. Central bank monthly remittance bulletins.
3. KNOMAD policy briefs.
4. National foreign-employment bureau data (where available).
5. Country-specific IPS-equivalent migration research.

**Data quality framing:** Formal-channel remittances are well-measured; informal-channel flows are estimated. Diaspora population estimates vary by methodology (foreign-born residents vs ethnically-identified vs naturalization paths).

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The remittance-resilience trap*: remittances are usually more resilient than other capital flows, but this resilience has limits (Gulf labor market shocks, exchange-rate-driven shifts).
- *The diaspora-investment-potential trap*: diaspora investment is real but conditional on confidence (legal protection, ease of business). Citing potential without conditions is hand-waving.
- *The political-diaspora trap*: treating politically-active diasporas as homogeneous when they often have internal political diversity.

## Section 14: Sanctions Exposure

**Central question:** *"What sanctions exposures does the country face, and what does that imply for transaction risk and compliance overhead?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Lists relevant sanctions regimes by jurisdiction (US OFAC, EU, UK, UN) with current status.
- Names specific sanctioned entities or individuals when relevant to portfolio decisions.
- Secondary sanctions risk: how third-party engagement with sanctioned parties affects home-country institutions.
- Recent changes: removals, additions, license issuance, enforcement actions.
- Banking-channel implications: which banks process which transactions.

**Signs of weak content:**
- *Generic "sanctions complicate engagement"*: hedging without specifics.
- *Stale regime citations*: citing sanctions regimes without recent removals or additions.
- *Missing secondary-sanctions risk*: focusing on primary sanctions while ignoring secondary risk to non-US institutions.

**Source priority:**
1. US Treasury OFAC sanctions program pages (specific sanctions, recent designations).
2. EU sanctions consolidated list.
3. UK Office of Financial Sanctions Implementation (OFSI).
4. UN Security Council sanctions committees.
5. Recent OFAC enforcement actions.

**Data quality framing:** Sanctions regimes change quickly; cite vintage explicitly.

**When to omit Section 14:** Countries with no meaningful sanctions exposure (most of the list) can omit. Include when sanctions are part of the analytical landscape (Syria pre-2025, Iran, Russia, Sudan partial).

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The "sanctions-relief = normalization" trap*: removal of US primary sanctions doesn't automatically restore banking channels (de-risking persists).
- *The OFAC-only trap*: focusing only on US sanctions while ignoring EU, UK, UN regimes that may have different scope.
- *The compliance-cost trap*: underestimating the compliance overhead even for "low risk" sanctions-adjacent transactions.

## Section 15: Market Entry

**Central question:** *"What are the actual entry conditions for foreign investment, and what's the operating reality vs the policy framework?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Distinguishes formal framework (BOI, FTZs, investment treaties) from operating reality (bureaucratic friction, political risk, regulatory unpredictability).
- Sectoral entry matrix: which sectors are open, restricted, or sensitive.
- Recent specific case studies (Adani disputes for Sri Lanka) as instructive examples for new entrants.
- Tax framework, repatriation rules, ownership restrictions in concrete terms.
- Doing Business equivalent metrics with caveats about methodology changes.

**Signs of weak content:**
- *Policy-framework-without-operating-reality*: citing the formal investment regime as if it determined outcomes.
- *Sectoral generality*: "energy is open" without addressing the political constraints visible in Adani disputes.
- *Missing recent cases*: not learning from visible recent foreign-investor experiences in the country.

**Source priority:**
1. Country Board of Investment / equivalent.
2. World Bank Doing Business (now suspended but historical data useful).
3. OECD Investment Policy Reviews where available.
4. UNCTAD World Investment Report country chapters.
5. AmCham / EuroCham country chapters' business climate reports.
6. Recent specific investor disputes / case studies.

**Data quality framing:** Formal framework data is well-documented. Operating-reality data is more anecdotal and reflects specific investor experiences.

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The reform-rhetoric trap*: governments announce business-environment reforms regularly; implementation lags. Distinguish announcement from implementation.
- *The single-investor-story trap*: one large investor's bad experience doesn't define the entire investment climate, but is an instructive data point.
- *The sectoral-blanket trap*: applying generic "open" / "restricted" labels when sectoral conditions vary by sub-sector.

## Section 16: Regulatory Regime

**Central question:** *"What's the regulatory architecture for the sector most relevant to portfolio activity, and how predictable is regulatory application?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Sector-specific regulatory framework (financial services, energy, telecommunications, etc.) appropriate to portfolio focus.
- Names the actual regulators with current leadership.
- Recent regulatory actions: license issuance, license revocations, enforcement actions.
- Distinguishes regulatory architecture (the rules on paper) from regulatory application (how rules are actually enforced).
- Independence of regulators dimension: are sectoral regulators captured or independent?

**Signs of weak content:**
- *All-regulators-listed*: cataloging all regulators rather than focusing on those relevant to the portfolio.
- *Static framework description*: rules-as-written without enforcement reality.
- *Missing independence assessment*: regulatory architecture without addressing capture risk.

**Source priority:**
1. Country sectoral regulators' websites and recent publications.
2. World Bank / IFC sector-specific assessments.
3. Industry association reports.
4. Recent regulatory rulings, enforcement actions.

**Data quality framing:** Regulatory frameworks are well-documented; enforcement reality is more anecdotal.

**When to merge with Section 15:** Often Section 16 can be folded into Section 15 (Market Entry) when regulatory analysis is shallow. Keep separate when the regulatory dimension is analytically central (financial-services-heavy portfolios in jurisdictions with complex regulation).

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The independence-claim trap*: regulators that are formally independent (central banks especially) can be operationally constrained.
- *The new-law trap*: recently enacted regulations may not yet be enforced (Sri Lanka's Online Safety Act between enactment and enforcement).
- *The unified-regulator trap*: treating "the regulator" as monolithic when different functions (licensing, supervision, enforcement) may have different operational realities.

## Section 17: Project Operating Environment

**Central question:** *"What are the practical operating conditions for projects in this country, including infrastructure, skills, security, and logistics?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Specific infrastructure assessment relevant to the portfolio activity (ports, airports, power reliability, internet/telecom, road network).
- Skilled-labor availability and cost.
- Operational security risk (different from security & stability section — this is "what does it cost to operate safely?").
- Banking and treasury operations: which banks are reliable, what FX operations look like.
- Procurement and supply chain considerations.

**Signs of weak content:**
- *Infrastructure-list-without-prioritization*: cataloging all infrastructure when only some matters for the portfolio.
- *Generic operating-environment*: not connecting operating conditions to specific project types.
- *Missing cost dimension*: qualitative-only assessment without cost implications.

**Source priority:**
1. World Bank Country Economic Memorandum (logistics, infrastructure dimensions).
2. World Bank Logistics Performance Index.
3. ITU country statistics for telecommunications.
4. AmCham operational climate reports.
5. ICRC / UN Security Risk Management for security operating conditions.

**Data quality framing:** Operational data is highly specific to use case; treat general assessments as starting points.

**When to omit Section 17:** Early-stage country engagement where specific operating conditions are premature.

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The capital-city-projection trap*: operating conditions in Colombo or Khartoum are not the operating conditions in Mannar or Darfur.
- *The headline-infrastructure trap*: focusing on flagship infrastructure (Colombo Port) while missing the network conditions (secondary roads, regional electricity) that matter for project implementation.

## Section 18: Comparative Benchmarking

**Central question:** *"How does this country compare to relevant peers on dimensions material to the portfolio decision, and what does that comparison tell us?"*

**Signs of strong content:**
- Explicit statement of *purpose* for comparison: trajectory analogue (Sri Lanka vs Ghana on post-default recovery)? size-similar peer (Sri Lanka vs Bangladesh on regional positioning)? Sectoral analogue (Morocco vs Egypt on tourism-dependent macro)?
- 4-6 comparator countries selected systematically based on the comparison's purpose.
- Indicators chosen because they're decision-relevant, not because they're standard.
- Comparison with analytical conclusion, not just side-by-side table.

**Signs of weak content (failures observed):**
- *Arbitrary comparator selection*: comparators chosen because they "feel right" without justifying why. (Most of my briefs.)
- *Indicator-soup*: comparing across 15 indicators without identifying which 3-4 are most material.
- *Table-without-analysis*: showing the comparison table without drawing the analytical conclusion.
- *Self-justifying comparison*: comparator set chosen to flatter the country (only comparing Sri Lanka to weaker performers).

**Source priority:**
1. World Bank / IMF data for cross-country comparison.
2. Same-source datasets for indicators (avoid mixing WB and IMF figures for the same indicator).
3. Recent sectoral comparisons from specialized institutions (IFC for investment climate; CGD for development).

**Data quality framing:** Cross-country comparison requires same methodology; mixed sources produce misleading comparisons.

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The flattering-comparator trap*: choosing comparators that make the country look better than it is.
- *The indicator-noise trap*: aggregating indicators that move differently obscures meaningful differences.
- *The peer-vs-aspiration confusion trap*: comparing to current peers when the relevant comparison is aspirational peers (countries the analyzed country wants to become).

## Section 19: Outlook (12-24 months)

**Central question:** *"What is the most likely trajectory over the next 18-24 months, what are the alternative trajectories, and what would we need to see to update?"*

This is consistently the strongest section across all five briefs because the scenario structure imposes analytical discipline. Use it as a template for what every section should aspire to.

**Signs of strong content:**
- 3 scenarios: base case (most likely), plausible alternative (significant probability), downside tail (low probability, high impact).
- Explicit probability statements for each scenario. The probabilities should sum to ~100%; if not, that's a signal that scenario space is incomplete.
- Each scenario has explicit *drivers* (what specifically produces this outcome) and *implications* (what it means for portfolio decisions).
- Scenarios are differentiated enough that they require different portfolio responses.
- Updates triggers: what specific events/data would shift assessment toward each scenario.

**Signs of weak content:**
- *Generic optimism/pessimism*: scenarios that are vague "things go better" or "things go worse."
- *Scenarios without drivers*: outcomes asserted without causal chains.
- *Probability-without-grounding*: numerical probabilities that aren't justified by the scenario structure.
- *Missing decision implication*: scenarios without portfolio-action translation.
- *Identical scenarios*: three scenarios that all look similar with minor variations.

**Source priority:** Outlook is synthesis of the rest of the brief; sources are the same as the upstream sections.

**Data quality framing:** Outlook is explicitly forward-looking and uncertain. The role of the section is to make uncertainty structured, not to pretend it doesn't exist.

**Common analytical traps:**
- *The "most likely = highly likely" trap*: a 55% probability base case is the most likely scenario but is far from certain. Strong scenarios maintain this nuance.
- *The optimism-pessimism symmetry trap*: assuming upside and downside scenarios should be symmetric. Often they're not (Sri Lanka's downside tail involves second sovereign default; upside is gradual normalization — not symmetric).
- *The black-swan-coverage trap*: trying to cover every conceivable downside ends up covering none well. Three scenarios is the right resolution.
- *The "all-scenarios" hedge*: producing scenarios that collectively cover all outcomes is hedging, not analysis. Scenarios should commit to a likely trajectory.

## Cross-cutting principles

Three principles apply across every section:

**1. Describe-vs-analyze.** A description tells the reader what the institutional structure is. An analysis tells the reader what that structure implies for a portfolio decision. Strong sections analyze; weak sections describe. Test: "Could I delete this paragraph and the analytical content of the brief would be unchanged?" If yes, the paragraph is description.

**2. Data quality framing.** Every section involving quantitative claims should signal data confidence. Not as a separate disclaimer but as integrated framing: "Sri Lanka's CBSL produces internationally-validated monthly data; figures here can be treated with confidence." vs "Sudan's macro figures are best-guess estimates with substantial uncertainty given conflict coverage gaps."

**3. Operational translation.** Analytical content should connect to portfolio/operational implications. A section that's analytically rich but operationally disconnected leaves the reader without a clear "so what." Strong sections embed the operational implication; weak sections leave it implicit.

The fundamental test for any section: **a senior reader scanning the brief should be able to identify the analytical takeaway in 30 seconds.** If they have to read carefully to extract the analytical conclusion, the section is too descriptive.
